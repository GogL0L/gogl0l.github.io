Proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus states that if a function \(f\) is continuous over the interval \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on \([a, b]\), then:

\[\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)\]

Proof:

Let’s consider \(F(x)\) as the area under the curve of \(f(t)\) from \(a\) to \(x\), where \(a \leq x \leq b\). So, by definition:

\[F(x) = \int_{a}^{x} f(t) dt\]

Now, let’s consider \(h\) as a small change in \(x\), then the change in \(F(x)\), which is \(F(x+h) - F(x)\), can be given as:

\[F(x+h) - F(x) = \int_{a}^{x+h} f(t) dt - \int_{a}^{x} f(t) dt = \int_{x}^{x+h} f(t) dt\]

As \(h\) approaches \(0\), the value of \(\int_{x}^{x+h} f(t) dt\) approaches \(f(x)h\). Therefore, the derivative of \(F(x)\) with respect to \(x\) is \(f(x)\):

\[F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h} = f(x)\]

This proves the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
